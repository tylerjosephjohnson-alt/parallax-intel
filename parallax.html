How the evidence score works — full methodology
This is not a truth score
Parallax does not tell you what is true. It maps what is known, what is contested, what is unknown, and who benefits from each version. The score is a structured uncertainty profile — the lower it is, the less evidence currently exists to evaluate the claim. A low score does not mean a story is false. It means it is insufficiently evidenced to act on confidently.
The 5 scoring dimensions
1 of 5 Domain evidence
Max 40 pts
Checks 8 independent domains — military, satellite, narrative, population, logistics, energy, financial, legal — for data that corroborates or contradicts the story's core claims. Physical evidence (satellite imagery, equipment records, financial data) outweighs statements from any party.
Confirmed signal
Physical evidence exists and is independently verifiable — satellite image, photo-verified equipment, primary document, legal filing
Contested signal
Two or more sources with conflicting claims and no independent physical verification to resolve them
Unknown signal
No data available in this domain — not the same as the claim being false, just not yet evidenced
Data void
Active information blackout — journalists banned, satellite blocked, internet shut down. A void is itself a signal, not just absence of data
2 of 5 Source independence
Max 25 pts
Counts how many genuinely independent sources corroborate the core claims — not just how many sources report the story. Independence requires: different owner, different funding, different country base, different editorial tradition. Ten outlets owned by the same company citing each other count as one source, not ten.
Source-count inflation — the most common misleading signal in news coverage
When 14 outlets all cite the same government statement, that is 1 original claim amplified 14 times — not 14 independent verifications. Parallax identifies this pattern and penalises the score accordingly. The penalty is called single-source amplification and applies −15 points when detected.
3 of 5 Motive and interest
Max 15 pts
Every source has interests. Showing who benefits from a claim being believed does not mean the claim is false — it means the reader has the context to weigh it correctly. Sources with direct financial or political interests in a story's outcome are weighted differently from sources with no stake.
Example: A pharmaceutical company's study on its own drug is different from an independent university study. Both may be accurate — but the motive context is essential information. Parallax displays funding sources and ownership chains on every source card so readers can make this judgement themselves.
4 of 5 Coordination detection
Max 10 pts
Detects coordinated narrative manufacturing — not individual false claims, but organised patterns. Organic news spreads in waves from primary sources. Coordinated operations launch simultaneously across unrelated outlets.
Timing sync
Abnormal burst — many outlets publishing same framing within minutes of each other
Language match
Near-identical phrasing across ostensibly independent outlets — similarity score above 0.85
Pattern library
Match against 200+ documented operation signatures — Storm-1516, Doppelganger, and others
5 of 5 Time and completeness
Max 10 pts
How old is the story, and how complete is the picture? Both matter. Early reports are structurally unreliable. Stories missing key perspectives are incomplete even if all available facts are accurate.
The early report automatic discount — explained
Research across thousands of major breaking news events shows that initial reports are wrong on at least one key detail — perpetrator identity, casualty numbers, location, cause — in approximately 70% of cases within the first 6 hours. This is not because journalists are dishonest. It is because events are inherently unclear as they unfold, sources have incomplete information, and there is commercial pressure to publish fast.

What the confidence labels mean
Low confidence
Insufficient evidence to evaluate the claim reliably. May be breaking news with no physical verification, a story from an information blackout zone, or a claim where only interested parties have spoken. Do not share or act on this story until evidence develops.
Medium confidence
Some independent corroboration exists but key domains remain contested or unknown. The broad picture may be reliable while specific details remain uncertain. Treat with appropriate scepticism — note what remains unverified.
High confidence
Multiple independent domain signals corroborate the core claims. Primary evidence (satellite, photo-verified, legal filings) present. Story is more than 24 hours old with consistent evidence. Core facts are reliable — individual details may still vary by source.
A note on what these labels are not
None of these labels mean a story is true or false. High confidence means the evidence picture is well-established, not that every claim is proven. Low confidence means the evidence is thin, not that the story is fabricated. Parallax shows you the evidence map. You draw the conclusions.
Why we built scoring this way
Every other news scoring system produces a single number and tells you to trust it. We think that is the wrong approach — it replaces one form of epistemic authority with another, and a system that tells people what to think is just another narrative controller with better data.

Our goal is to make you a better analyst, not a more compliant one. Every score is fully explainable — you can see every signal, every penalty, every bonus, and every source that contributed to it. If you think we got something wrong, you can tell us and we will update it publicly. The correction log is a first-class feature, not an afterthought.

The scoring system will be wrong sometimes. What matters is that it is honest about uncertainty, correctable when wrong, and always shows its working.

The International Criminal Court has existing arrest warrants for war crimes in Darfur from the 2003–2008 conflict, but no enforcement mechanism exists. What is happening in El Fasher has the structural characteristics of a deliberate siege designed to starve a population into submission — a pattern documented extensively in the earlier Darfur conflict that resulted in ICC indictments.

HIGH

The US-Iran war and associated Strait of Hormuz crisis has produced the largest single disruption to global oil supply since the 1973 Arab oil embargo. Before the war began on 28 February 2026, more than 100 vessels per day transited the strait, carrying approximately 21 million barrels of oil — 20% of global supply. As of 15 April, the Abu Dhabi National Oil Company (ADNOC) CEO Sultan Al Jaber stated that 230 loaded oil tankers remain stranded inside the Gulf, unable to exit.

Brent crude peaked at $102–$104/barrel following the announcement of the US naval blockade on 13 April — a gain of approximately 40% since the war began. WTI (West Texas Intermediate) hit $104/barrel. Prices have since eased to $90–$94/barrel as the White House signalled optimism about a second round of peace talks. The International Monetary Fund on 15 April cut its 2026 global growth forecast from 3.3% to 3.1%, warning of an "adverse scenario" in which oil prices remain near $100/barrel.

US stock markets have responded counterintuitively, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq Composite hitting fresh record highs on Wednesday on ceasefire deal optimism, recovering all losses from the war. In contrast, most Asian and European indices are trading lower. Wholesale gas prices in the United States have risen approximately $1.20/gallon since the war began, with the national average reaching $4.12/gallon. Iran's National Petrochemical Company has suspended all exports until further notice to prioritise domestic supply disrupted by the strikes.

Why markets diverged from physical reality
Stock markets are pricing in a deal scenario — the war ends, the strait reopens, oil normalises. Physical oil markets reflect actual supply disruption. The gap between the two represents the market's assessment that a deal is more likely than not. If talks collapse, the stock market correction could be severe.
Significance
The 1973 Arab oil embargo reduced supply by approximately 7% — causing a 300% price increase and contributing to a global recession. The current disruption has removed 20%+ of global supply from the strait, though Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iraq have attempted to reroute through alternative pipelines. The East-West Pipeline across Saudi Arabia (Petroline) can carry ~5M barrels/day — roughly 25% of normal Hormuz capacity. Saudi Aramco's spare capacity has been deployed. The gap between pre-war and current supply is partially offset, but not fully. Most economic modelling suggests every $10/barrel increase in oil prices reduces global GDP by approximately 0.1–0.2 percentage points.

MEDIUM

On 15 April 2026, Pakistan's Chief of Army Staff General Asim Munir arrived in Tehran carrying what Al Jazeera described as a "new message from Washington," in the latest effort to revive stalled US–Iran peace negotiations before the two-week ceasefire expires on 22 April. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi met with Munir and subsequently told state media that Tehran "remains committed to promoting peace," while warning about the "dangerous consequences of US positions and provocative actions in the Persian Gulf."

The first round of talks, held in Islamabad on 11–12 April under Pakistani mediation, marked the most significant direct engagement between the United States and Iran in decades. The talks lasted more than 20 hours and included both indirect and direct exchanges. According to Al Jazeera and Reuters, the discussions focused on Iran's nuclear programme, sanctions relief, Iran's frozen assets estimated at $6–10 billion, and control of the Strait of Hormuz. They concluded without a memorandum of understanding. Vice President JD Vance said Iran had not provided an "affirmative commitment" not to develop nuclear weapons. Iran's parliamentary speaker Ghalibaf said Washington had failed to earn the delegation's trust.

The White House has since told CNN it feels "good about prospects of a deal" and that Vance is expected to lead a second round if one can be arranged. Trump told a New York Post reporter in Islamabad: "You should stay there, really, because something could be happening over the next two days." However, Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif has left on a four-day trip to Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey — apparently complicating the timeline. UN Secretary General António Guterres said it is "highly probable" that talks will restart.

Why medium confidence — key uncertainty
The White House optimism and Trump's hints of imminent talks have not been matched by concrete announcements of time, place, or agenda. Iran says it will participate to "expose US behaviour" rather than because it trusts the process. The timeline pressure of 22 April creates both urgency and the risk of talks being announced and collapsing quickly. Multiple competing back-channels (Pakistan, Russia's uranium offer, China's diplomatic engagement) may complicate rather than accelerate resolution.
Significance — why Pakistan as mediator matters
Pakistan's role as the primary US–Iran intermediary is unprecedented. Pakistan maintains diplomatic relations with both countries, hosts a significant Shia population with cultural ties to Iran, and depends on Gulf Arab states (Saudi Arabia, UAE) for remittances and financial support. It is simultaneously undergoing its own IMF-conditioned economic stabilisation programme, giving it incentive to restore regional stability. Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif's concurrent diplomatic tour of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Turkey suggests Pakistan is also seeking guarantees and support from regional powers for whatever framework emerges.
Russia's uranium offer — a complicating variable
Kremlin spokesman Peskov confirmed on 15 April that Russia has repeated its offer to accept Iran's enriched uranium as part of a potential deal. This would theoretically address the US demand to prevent Iran's enrichment programme from producing weapons-grade material, while preserving Iran's civilian nuclear capability. The offer creates a potential face-saving mechanism for Iran but requires US acceptance of a Russia-mediated nuclear arrangement — which the US has not publicly endorsed.

Snowball chain — how religious persecution escalates
Stage 1 — Legal discrimination and dehumanisation
Laws restrict religious practice, employment, or property rights. State narratives frame the group as a security threat or cultural contamination. This stage can last decades — German Jews 1933-1938, Rohingya 1982-2017. It is the stage where intervention is cheapest and most effective.
Stage 2 — Active state suppression + first refugee outflows
Mass arrests of leaders. Forced closure of worship sites. Destruction of religious heritage. First significant refugee outflows begin — this is the earliest visible humanitarian signal and the point where neighbouring states start experiencing pressure.
Stage 3 — Systematic violence + regional destabilisation
Detention camps, pogroms, organised killings. Mass cross-border displacement. Receiving countries face economic and political stress — Lebanon with Syrian Christians and Shia, Bangladesh with 1M+ Rohingya, Iraqi Kurdistan with Yazidis. The persecution becomes a regional geopolitical event, not just a domestic one.
Stage 4 — Armed conflict, proxy intervention, or genocide
Persecuted group or external actors take up arms. Foreign powers intervene — often for geopolitical rather than humanitarian reasons. This is the stage that generates TruthLens news stories. But the root cause began at Stage 1 often years earlier. Stage 4 is the snowball reaching the valley floor.
Stage 5 — International accountability (rare, slow)
ICC indictments, UN resolutions, truth and reconciliation. Typically decades after the original persecution. Rwanda 1994 — ICTR completed 2015. Srebrenica 1995 — Mladic convicted 2017. The lag between persecution and accountability is itself a signal about the limits of the international system.

Snowball chain — how an economic collapse propagates
This is not a prediction that a collapse will occur. It is a map of the transmission mechanism if the elevated stress indicators above deteriorate simultaneously. Understanding the chain in advance allows earlier intervention at each stage.
Trigger event — CRE loan wave + Fed overtightening
Commercial real estate loan maturities force regional bank write-downs
$400B in CRE loans mature in a single quarter. Offices worth 50% less than book value. Banks must mark to market. Several regional banks become technically insolvent. FDIC action required. Media coverage triggers bank run psychology.
Week 1–2 · Credit markets seize
HY credit spreads spike from 432bps to 900bps+ · Corporate borrowing costs double
Banks stop lending. Corporate bond market freezes. Companies with debt maturing cannot refinance. Private equity portfolio companies under immediate stress. VIX spikes to 60+. Equity markets fall 15–25% in two weeks.
Month 1–3 · Consumer and housing
Consumer confidence collapses · Housing transaction freeze deepens · Layoffs begin
Consumer confidence index cliff-drops. Discretionary spending collapses. Retail and hospitality layoffs begin. Housing market — already frozen — becomes completely illiquid. Home prices begin nominal declines in overextended markets (Phoenix, Austin, Miami).
Month 3–6 · Global contagion
Emerging market capital flight · Turkey, Argentina, Egypt CDS blow out · HSBC China exposure triggers EU banking stress
Global risk-off triggers EM capital flight. Countries with high USD debt (Turkey, Pakistan, Egypt) face dollar shortage. EU banks exposed to EM (BBVA → Turkey, BNP → MENA) report losses. European banking stress echoes through sovereign bond spreads. ECB forced to activate OMT (Outright Monetary Transactions).
Month 6–18 · Policy response
Fed emergency rate cuts · Fiscal stimulus · IMF emergency programmes for EM · Recovery begins
Central banks globally cut rates to zero or negative. Fiscal stimulus packages debated in legislatures. IMF deploys emergency facilities to EM. Recovery trajectory depends on speed of policy response and whether financial system contagion is contained before becoming self-reinforcing. Historical: 2008 GFC recovery took 6 years to reach pre-crisis employment. COVID recovery took 2 years due to faster, larger policy response.
This chain is conditional — current probability estimate: 18–25% over 24 months
The triggers above require simultaneous deterioration across multiple currently-elevated indicators. The most likely scenario remains a mild recession (probability: 45%) with contained financial system stress — not a 2008-scale event. The chain above requires the CRE problem to become acute, credit markets to freeze, AND consumer stress to accelerate simultaneously. Each link reduces the probability. The chain is shown so you understand what to watch for and where intervention is possible.
